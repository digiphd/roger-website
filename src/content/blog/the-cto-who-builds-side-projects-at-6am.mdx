It's 5:47 AM. My alarm went off two minutes ago. The house is dark, the coffee hasn't kicked in, and I'm already reviewing pull requests that my AI agents submitted overnight.

By 6:30, I'll have shipped a feature, dispatched three more agent tasks, and made at least one product decision for [Axislabs](https://axislabs.dev). By 9, I'll switch context entirely and spend the next eight hours as CTO of the company I co-founded.

This is my life. I run a full-time CTO role and build a portfolio of AI-native SaaS products on the side. I've been doing this for five years. It's not glamorous. It's not always smart. But I genuinely believe it's one of the best decisions I've made.

This post is for every technical founder with a day job who feels guilty about their side project, or guilty about not having one.

## Why a CTO needs side projects

There's a paradox in technical leadership. The higher you climb, the less you build. Your calendar fills with 1:1s, architecture reviews, hiring loops, and stakeholder meetings. The craft that got you here slowly atrophies.

Side projects fix that.

<Highlight emoji="🔧">
Side projects are the gym for technical leaders. Your day job is the game. But you don't stay sharp by only playing games.
</Highlight>

But staying sharp is just the start. Here's what side projects actually give you:

**Optionality.** The tech industry laid off [over 260,000 workers in 2023](https://layoffs.fyi/) alone, and 2024 wasn't much better. Your CTO title doesn't make you immune. A portfolio of products generating even modest revenue is the difference between "I need a job immediately" and "I can take my time."

**Wealth outside salary.** Tech salaries are strong: the [median for computer and IT occupations hit $105,990 in 2024](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/computer-and-information-technology/). But salary growth is slowing. The [2025 Dice Tech Salary Report](https://www.dice.com/technologists/ebooks/tech-salary-report/salary-trends.html) found average increases dropped to 1.6%, down from 3.5% just two years prior. Salary alone is a ceiling. Products are leverage.

**Real-world R&D.** At my day job, I have to be responsible. Production systems, real users, real consequences. Side projects let me experiment with new tools, frameworks, and AI capabilities without risking anyone's livelihood.

And I'm not alone. The [Stack Overflow 2024 Developer Survey](https://survey.stackoverflow.co/2024/) found that roughly 70% of professional developers code outside of work as a hobby. We're wired for this.

## The companies that started as side projects

If you need permission to take your side project seriously, here it is: some of the biggest tech companies in history started exactly where you are.

**Twitter** was built during a hackathon at a failing podcast company. Jack Dorsey prototyped it in two weeks. **Slack** started as an internal chat tool at a gaming startup that was going nowhere. Stewart Butterfield's gaming company failed; the side project became a [$27 billion company](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/twitter-slack-eight-other-side-192537379.html). **Gmail** came out of Google's 20% time policy. **Craigslist** was literally an email list Craig Newmark sent to his friends.

<Callout type="info" title="The hybrid founder advantage">
A [University of Wisconsin study](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.2013.1882) found that founders who kept their day jobs were 33% less likely to fail than those who quit to go full-time. The safety net doesn't kill ambition. It funds patience.
</Callout>

The mythology says you need to quit your job and go all in. The data says the opposite. Keeping your day job is a strategic advantage, not a sign you're not serious enough.

## The 6AM routine

People romanticize morning routines. Mine isn't romantic. It's functional.

<Step number={1} title="5:45 AM: Alarm, coffee, zero scrolling">
No email, no Twitter, no Slack. The phone stays face down. I have maybe 60-90 minutes of peak cognitive capacity before my day job starts, and I refuse to spend it on other people's priorities.
</Step>

<Step number={2} title="5:55 AM: Review overnight agent work">
My AI agents run tasks while I sleep: writing tests, drafting copy, scaffolding features, researching competitors. I review their output, approve what's good, flag what's not, and dispatch new tasks.
</Step>

<Step number={3} title="6:15 AM: One decision, one build">
I pick one product from my Axislabs portfolio and make one meaningful decision or ship one thing. Not two. Not three. One. Context switching across multiple products before 7 AM is a recipe for doing nothing well.
</Step>

<Step number={4} title="7:00 AM: Stop. Switch context.">
Hard stop. Shower, get ready, mentally transition to my CTO role. The side projects don't exist until tomorrow morning.
</Step>

There's real science behind why this works. Research published in [Frontiers in Neuroscience](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnins.2022.995452/full) shows that cortisol peaks 30-45 minutes after waking, increasing 38-75% above baseline. This "cortisol awakening response" primes your brain for alertness and focused work. A [systematic review in Sleep and Breathing](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11325-023-02895-0) found that cognitive performance varies by 9-34% depending on time of day, with attention and mental arithmetic peaking in morning hours.

<Highlight emoji="⏰">
Your best hour of the day is the first one. Spending it on email is like using a power drill to stir coffee.
</Highlight>

Roy Baumeister's research on willpower suggests that self-control [operates like a muscle that fatigues with use](https://faculty.washington.edu/jdb/345/345%20Articles/Baumeister%20et%20al.%20(1998).pdf). You have the most of it in the morning, before a full day of decisions depletes it. (The original "ego depletion" model [faced replication challenges](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352250X24000952), but more recent research has refined rather than overturned it.) By 6 PM, after eight hours of CTO decisions, I'm not building anything good. I know this. I've tried.

## It's not time management. It's energy management.

This is the thing nobody tells you about side projects with a day job. The bottleneck isn't time. You can find an hour. The bottleneck is energy.

I tried building at night for two years. After a full day of architecture decisions, code reviews, and team leadership, I'd sit down at 9 PM and stare at my editor. The code I wrote at night was mediocre. The decisions I made were lazy. I'd spend an hour, produce garbage, and feel worse than if I'd just watched TV.

Mornings changed everything. Not because I found more time. Because I found better energy.

The pattern I've settled on: mornings for creative work (building, product decisions, writing). Day job for leadership and execution. Evenings for recovery. Weekends mostly off. "Mostly" is doing heavy lifting in that sentence, but I'm honest about it.

## AI agents as a force multiplier

Here's what makes this possible in 2026 in a way it wasn't in 2021: AI agents.

I have a team of AI agents that work while I sleep. They're not perfect. They're not autonomous. But they let me operate like a small team when I'm actually one person with 60 minutes before breakfast.

Here's what my agents handle:
- Writing and running test suites
- Drafting marketing copy and social posts
- Scaffolding new features from specs I write
- Researching competitors and market data
- Code reviews on my own PRs (a second pair of eyes, even artificial ones, catches things)

About 60% of agent output is usable without edits. Another 25% needs light revision. The rest gets scrapped. That's still a massive multiplier. Instead of doing everything myself in 60 minutes, I review and direct in 60 minutes while agents handle the volume work.

<Callout type="tip" title="The human-agent workflow">
Think of it like managing junior developers. You don't write the code for them. You write clear specs, review their output, and course-correct. The leverage comes from directing, not doing.
</Callout>

If you're building side projects in 2026 without AI agents, you're choosing hard mode. Not as a flex. As a statement of fact.

## The honest downsides

This is where most "side project" posts stop. They give you the routine, the inspiration, the hack. They don't tell you what it actually costs.

I'll be honest.

**Burnout is real and it's close.** [83% of software developers report burnout](https://www.usehaystack.io/blog/83-of-developers-suffer-from-burnout-haystack-analytics-study-finds), and that's people with *one* job. A [CEREVITY survey](https://cerevity.com/tech-founder-burnout-statistics-2025-73-report-hidden-mental-health-crisis/) of tech founders found 73% experience what they call "shadow burnout," persistent exhaustion hidden behind high performance. I've been there. You push through because the work is exciting, and one day you realize you haven't had a real day off in three months.

**Context switching has a cost.** There's a [well-documented cognitive penalty](https://www.apa.org/topics/research/multitasking) for switching between tasks. Now imagine switching between entire *companies* every morning. Some days the transition is smooth. Some days I'm debugging a side project problem during a leadership meeting, or thinking about work architecture when I should be focused on Axislabs.

**Relationships feel it.** This one's harder to quantify. When you wake up at 5:45 every day to work, you're going to bed earlier than everyone else. Social events get harder. Your partner learns what "I just need to finish this one thing" really means (it means an hour, minimum). I don't have a clean answer for this one. I manage it. Some weeks better than others.

If you're going to do this, go in with your eyes open. The 6 AM routine sounds disciplined on a blog post. In practice, it means being tired at dinner, saying no to things you want to say yes to, and occasionally questioning whether any of it is worth it.

## My macro thesis (and why I keep going)

So why keep doing it? Because I believe we're in a specific window of opportunity, and it won't last forever.

The economy is shifting. AI is restructuring entire industries. [Tech salary growth is decelerating](https://www.dice.com/technologists/ebooks/tech-salary-report/salary-trends.html). Layoffs are normalized. The implicit contract of "work hard at one company and you'll be fine" is broken, and I think most people know it even if they haven't acted on it yet.

I believe there's a 3-4 year window right now where technical builders who can ship AI-native products have an outsized advantage. The tools are mature enough to build real things. The market hasn't been fully captured yet. Incumbents are slow. And if you can build while keeping a salary, you can take bigger swings because you're not burning runway.

<Highlight emoji="🎯">
The goal isn't to replace my salary. The goal is to build enough product revenue that my salary becomes optional. That's the definition of freedom for a technical founder.
</Highlight>

That's what FU money actually means. Not yachts. Not retiring at 35. Just the ability to walk away from any single income source without panic. A portfolio of products, even small ones, gets you closer to that than a 1.6% annual raise ever will.

## Practical tips if you're doing this too

If you're a developer or technical leader building on the side, here's what I've learned the hard way:

<Step number={1} title="Protect the morning block like it's sacred">
No meetings, no email, no Slack before your creative work is done. Tell your team you're unavailable before 9 AM. Nobody will question it.
</Step>

<Step number={2} title="Pick one project at a time">
A portfolio is built sequentially, not in parallel. Work on one product until it hits a milestone, then move to the next. Spreading yourself across three projects simultaneously means none of them ship.
</Step>

<Step number={3} title="Use AI agents for everything you can">
Set up agents to handle testing, research, drafting, and scaffolding. Review their output in the morning. Your job is to direct, not do. This turns 60 minutes into the equivalent of 3-4 hours of output.
</Step>

<Step number={4} title="Schedule recovery like you schedule work">
If it's not on the calendar, it doesn't happen. I block one full weekend day with zero work. No code, no Slack, no "just checking one thing." Your brain needs downtime to do creative work. [Research on burnout](https://leaddev.com/culture/engineering-burnout-rising-2025-layoffs-reshape-tech-industry) consistently shows that recovery isn't optional; 68% of tech workers reported burnout symptoms in 2024, up from 49% three years prior.
</Step>

<Step number={5} title="Tell someone what you're building">
Don't build in silence. Ship in public. Post updates. Write about your process. The audience you build while building is as valuable as the product itself. I learned this lesson [the hard way](/blog/five-products-zero-audience).
</Step>

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch for shadow burnout">
If you're still performing well but feel hollow, that's not discipline. That's shadow burnout. 73% of tech founders experience it. The fix isn't working harder. It's resting before you think you need to.
</Callout>

## The bottom line

I'm a CTO who builds side projects at 6 AM. Some mornings it feels like a superpower. Some mornings it feels like a problem. Most mornings it just feels like the thing I do.

If you're reading this and you have a side project collecting dust, or an idea you keep putting off because you "don't have time," here's what I'll say: you have the time. You might not have the energy, and that's a different problem with a different solution.

Start with one morning. Just one. Set the alarm 45 minutes early, make coffee, and build something. Don't check email. Don't open Slack. Just build.

You'll know pretty quickly whether this is for you. And if it is, welcome to the 6 AM club. The coffee is mediocre and the odds are long, but the company is good.
