AI changed everything about what it means to ship software. If you're still competing on "we have a better UI" or "we have a cool feature set", you lost 18 months ago and haven't noticed it yet.

Here's what happened, why it matters, and the six moats that still work.

## The Product Trap

For a decade, the SaaS playbook was simple: build a better product and people will come. The bottleneck was *execution* — good engineers, good design, good product sense were scarce resources.

That bottleneck collapsed.

AI made product building nearly free. Not literally free, but close enough that it no longer matters. A solo founder with Cursor and Claude can build what required a team of five in 2023. The gap between "I have an idea" and "I have a working product" has gone from months to days.

This means 80% of SaaS products now in development don't have a moat. They have a product. They'll ship someone will try them. But if a competitor appears with a better distribution engine — and they *will* — the weaker product with the stronger audience wins. Every time.

**Product is a commodity. Moats are the only thing left that matters.**

## What Actually Is a Moat?

A moat is something your competitors cannot easily copy. Not something that takes longer to do, something they *cannot* do.

I don't mean "hard to copy". Hard is just expensive. Moats are structural advantages — things that compound over time so that the longer someone operates with them, the harder it becomes for anyone else to catch up.

Here are the six that still work in 2026. I rank them by difficulty to copy, easiest to hardest.

---

## 1. Capital — The Funding Moat
**Difficulty to copy:** Moderate
**Works best for:** Well-funded startups, VC-backed companies

If you have more runway than your competitors, you can outlast them. Simple as that. Burn $12 million in customer acquisition, bleed for three years, acquire a market — that's a strategy if you have the capital.

But it's fragile. Capital is not a moat, it's a weapon. The second you stop spending, the moat disappears. Capital also comes with investors who want returns, which means you're trading freedom for time.

**My take:** If you're bootstrapping like I am, this moat doesn't apply to you. And that's fine, the rest are better.

**Who plays this game:** Linear, Notion, every Series B SaaS company burning cash to grow.

---

## 2. Distribution — The Audience Moat
**Difficulty to copy:** High
**Works best for:** Consumer tools, creator-focused products, any product where users discover through social/content

This is the moat most founders underestimate. Distribution means people hear about you before they know they need you.

Think about it: two competing products, one with a better feature set but zero audience. The other with a slightly inferior product and a hundred thousand followers who trust the founder. Which one wins first customers?

Distribution works differently depending on your market:

**B2C / creator products:** Social media, personal brand, TikTok, YouTube, short-form content. The founder becomes the distribution channel themselves. Think levels.io or Danny Postma.

**B2B products:** Partnerships, integrations, community, content marketing that speaks to a specific job title. Think of how Stripe won developers by being the API everyone else used.

The key insight: **distribution is earned over time, not bought at launch.** You can't turn on distribution like a faucet. You build it piece by piece, post by post, relationship by relationship.

At Axislabs, I run the content pipeline for all our products through a single personal brand: me. One raw video per day, distributed across TikTok, X, Instagram, Facebook. The AI handles the editing and repurposing. That's how a solo founder competes with funded teams.

**Who plays this game:** Pieter Levels, Danny Postma, anyone who built SaaS through content instead of ads.

---

## 3. Traction — The Momentum Moat
**Difficulty to copy:** High
**Works best for:** First movers, products with network effects

Once you have 10,000 users, a competitor with a better product and zero users is still at a disadvantage. Not because your product is better, because of momentum. Users trust what exists and what other people use.

Switching costs create natural friction. Even if your product is objectively worse, existing users face:

- The effort of learning a new tool
- The risk of data migration
- The fear of losing familiar workflows
- The inertia of "good enough"

The trap most people fall into: they think traction means revenue. It doesn't. Revenue is a lagging indicator of traction. Traction means attention, users, usage patterns, data points. Money eventually follows those things.

**Network effects** make this moat even stronger. If your product gets better as more people use it, each new user reinforces the moat for every existing user. Marketplaces benefit from this. Most SaaS products do not. If yours doesn't, don't pretend it does.

**Who plays this game:** Slack, Discord, Figma — products where the network *is* the product.

---

## 4. Community — The Engagement Moat
**Difficulty to copy:** High
**Works best for:** Developer tools, creative platforms, products where users help each other

A community is a self-sustaining ecosystem where users create value for other users. Your product is the venue, but the value comes from what people build together inside it.

Communities are powerful because they're self-reinforcing. Users answer each other's support questions. Users create tutorials for new users. Users share templates, plugins, integrations, and workflows. The product team doesn't have to create all the value. The community does.

This is why Notion's template marketplace matters more than their feature updates. Why Figma's community of designers sharing resources is a competitive advantage no feature parity can replicate. Why Stripe's developer community writes the documentation Stripe wishes it had.

Communities take years to build and minutes to destroy. You earn trust, then lose it. Treat your community like an afterthought and watch what happens.

**Who plays this game:** Notion, Figma, Stripe, Webflow, Supabase.

---

## 5. Indispensability — The Workflow Lock-In Moat
**Difficulty to copy:** Very High
**Works best for:** B2B SaaS, tools embedded in core business operations

This is the moat most B2B companies want: your product becomes so deeply embedded in how someone works that removing it would be catastrophic.

Think about your own toolkit. If X disappears tomorrow, what do you lose? For most people, the answer is "I'll have to rebuild my entire workflow". That's indispensability.

**There are three levels:**

**Level 1: Convenience.** Nice to have, but replaceable. Most SaaS products are here. This is not a moat.

**Level 2: Critical to a process.** Your product is part of a workflow that generates revenue or saves significant time. Replacing it means disruption and retraining. This is a good moat.

**Level 3: Indispensable.** Your product is the engine. Without it, the business stops or loses money. Nobody wants to replace that. This is the gold standard.

Most SaaS founders aim for Level 2 and never get there. The difference between a product people *use* and a product people *need* is the difference between churn and retention.

**Who plays this game:** Vercel, Linear, Intercom, any tool where leaving means rebuilding workflows.

---

## 6. Data IP — The Intelligence Moat
**Difficulty to copy:** Very High
**Works best for:** AI companies, analytics platforms, any product where proprietary data creates unique insights

This is the moat nobody talks about enough because it's the hardest to fake.

Most "data moats" are fake. Collecting user data is not a moat unless you can derive unique insights from it that your competitors cannot replicate with publicly available data.

The real data IP moat:

**It's not about volume.** Public data is cheap. Web scraping is commoditised. Having more data than everyone else means nothing if you're all analyzing the same thing the same way.

**It's about unique data.** Proprietary data collection that only you have access to. Partnerships that give you insights others don't have. Telemetry from a product that generates data nobody else can see.

**It's about the analysis.** Two companies can have identical datasets and come to completely opposite conclusions. The formulas, models, and relationships between datasets create the IP, not the raw numbers.

Think about it this way: what does your product know that nobody else knows, and does that knowledge give your users an advantage they can't get anywhere else? At Thoroughbreds.ai, we're building intelligence around horse racing performance. The raw data is available, but the predictive models, the relationships between datasets, the historical context, the proprietary metrics, that's our IP.

If you can't answer that question honestly, you don't have a data moat. You have a database.

**Who plays this game:** Thoroughbreds.ai (obviously), Google, Amazon, any AI company where training data quality is a competitive advantage.

## The Moat Hierarchy

Here's the thing most founders get wrong: they assume one moat is enough. It isn't. The strongest companies have multiple.

The hierarchy works like this:

**Tier 1:** Capital. Easy to get if you have connections. Easy to lose. Not recommended for bootstrappers.

**Tier 2:** Distribution + Traction. Distribution gets attention, traction converts it. Build in parallel. These are the most important moats for early-stage founders.

**Tier 3:** Community + Indispensability. Community compounds attention, indispensability reduces churn. Build these as you scale.

**Tier 4:** Data IP. Hardest to build, hardest to replicate, highest ceiling. Takes time but once you have it, this is the moat that actually matters.

**The trap:** building product first, moat later. That's backwards. The moat determines whether your product survives. Start with "how will people hear about this?", not "how do we build the best product?"

## Why 80% Will Flop

With AI making product building a commodity, the companies that survive won't be the ones with the best features. They'll be the ones with the strongest moats.

The 80% that flop won't fail because they couldn't build something. They'll fail because nobody heard about their product. Or they couldn't retain users once a better-distributed competitor appeared. Or they built something convenient instead of indispensable. Or they collected data but never turned it into insight.

The moat test is simple:

1. **Can a competitor build the same product in 30 days?** Yes? Your moat is product-only. Dead.
2. **Can a competitor buy their way into your market?** Yes? You need distribution or indispensability. Now.
3. **Do your customers need you, or just use you?** "Just use" means you're one feature away from being replaced.
4. **Does your proprietary data create unique insights?** If not, you're collecting data, not building a moat.
5. **Does your community create value without you?** If they can't, you have an audience, not a moat.

## What This Means for Me at Axislabs

We're building a portfolio of SaaS products. Every product needs to pass the moat test before we invest. Here's the filter:

- **Does it have a distribution channel we can own?** Yes? Build it. Personal brand, content, partnerships.
- **Does it solve a real problem deeply enough to become indispensable?** Yes? Build it. Focus on workflow integration, not features.
- **Does it generate unique data insights?** Yes? Build it. The data becomes the moat over time.
- **Is it just a better version of something that already exists?** No. Don't build it. The incumbent already has traction and you can't out-feature a moat.

The products in our portfolio — ClutchCut, PostDropr, EverContent, SilentAgents, TraceRaven, Webpipe — they all have to pass this test. Most will succeed on distribution alone. The ones that survive will survive on multiple moats.

The ones that don't, we'll kill. Fast. Revenue or nothing.
